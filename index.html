Find it in Modules

How do I find the directions in Modules?

Modules are the home base for this course. If you are confused, start there before emailing me. Most problems are solved by finding the exact assignment page and reading the directions once you are looking at the right thing.

  • Go to Modules and open the current week or unit.
  • Click the actual assignment, discussion, or quiz link inside the module.
  • On the assignment page, find what you are being asked to do, how you are being graded, and where to submit.
  • Check the due date in Canvas. Canvas is the source of truth for due dates.
  • If you still do not get it, narrow the question. Write down the assignment name, the part you do not understand, what you tried, and what you think the directions mean.

"I am confused" is not enough information for me to help quickly. Tell me which step you are stuck on and I can explain it in a different way.

Where do I start?

Start in Modules. That is the roadmap for this course.

If you are unsure what to do next, ask yourself:

  • What week or unit are we in?
  • What does the module say to do first?
  • What is the next submit or reply task in Canvas?

If you still feel lost after that, email me and tell me which module you are in and what step you got stuck on.

What do I do each week?

Each week you should expect some combination of readings, discussion work, and writing work.

Open Modules and work top to bottom. Do not skip ahead unless you are working ahead on purpose.

Where do I find due dates?

Due dates are in Canvas. Always use the date shown in Canvas as your source of truth.

If you are ever unsure, check the module for that week or unit, the Canvas calendar or to do list, and the assignment page itself.

Deadlines and extensions

Why are deadlines a big deal?

Deadlines are part of the skill in college. They are how you practice planning ahead, managing time, and meeting expectations.

In most workplaces, late work is not accepted. College is where you build the habit before the stakes get higher.

If you need help, communicate early and include a plan. That is what makes flexibility possible.

Can I get an extension?

Sometimes, yes.

  • If it is a planned conflict: work ahead. That is the expectation in college.
  • If it is unplanned: email me as soon as possible and include a plan.

If your message is sent after the deadline and does not include a plan, I may not be able to approve an extension.

What should I include in an extension email?
  • Your name and course
  • The assignment name
  • The reason, brief and simple
  • What you have completed so far
  • The date and time you will submit

Drafts and finals

How are drafts graded?

Drafts are graded on a complete or incomplete basis.

You will still see a rubric score out of 100 on your draft. That number is there to show what you would have earned if the draft were graded as a final. Use that score to decide how much revision you need before you submit your final.

Important note: if you submit your draft as your final without revision, your final score often ends up slightly lower than the draft rubric score. The draft score assumes you will revise.

What to do when you get draft feedback:

  • Read the rubric and look for where points were earned or lost
  • Read the in text annotations if they are included
  • Read the final comments at the end
  • Make a revision plan before you resubmit

If you want help understanding your feedback, you are welcome to email me or schedule office hours.

Office hours scheduling link: Calendly

What does 65 and below mean for drafts?

If you receive a score of 65 or below on a Narrative Essay draft rubric, you will be required to complete a Writing Center visit before your draft grade can be marked Complete.

To count, the visit must be a synchronous appointment, not an asynchronous document review. Synchronous means meeting with a tutor in real time, on Zoom or in person.

When booking, please select or mark that it is a required visit.

After your session, the Writing Center will email me your session notes or confirmation. Once I receive confirmation, I will update your draft grade to Complete.

If you have questions or need help booking an appointment, reach out as soon as possible.

How are finals graded?

Final essays receive a grade based on the rubric. The rubric shows where points were earned or lost.

Finals do not automatically receive long written feedback. If you would like more detailed feedback on your final essay, you are welcome to meet with me in an office hours appointment.

Office hours scheduling link: Calendly

Optional Writing Center boost for Narrative Essay finals (scores 65 and below)

If you received a score of 65 or below on the Narrative Essay final, you may complete a Writing Center visit to raise your score to a maximum of 70 (passing).

This is optional, but I strongly recommend it if you are below passing.

Submitting work and tech problems

Where do I submit assignments?

Submit work in the course where it is assigned. If the assignment is posted in ENGL&101, submit it in ENGL&101. If it is posted in ENGL117, submit it in ENGL117.

If you are unsure, open the assignment page and look for the submit area. If you still cannot find it, email me with the assignment name and a screenshot.

What if Canvas will not let me submit?

Try these steps:

  • Refresh the page and try again.
  • Try a different browser.
  • Try from a computer if you are on a phone.
  • Take a screenshot of the error message.

If it still does not work, email me as soon as possible and include the screenshot and the assignment name.

Discussions and annotations

What are the discussion requirements?

For most discussions, you should plan to complete one initial post and two replies to classmates. If you do fewer than two replies, the discussion may be marked incomplete.

What counts as a good reply?

A strong reply usually does at least one of these:

  • Responds to something specific the person said
  • Adds a new example or connection
  • Asks a thoughtful question that moves the conversation forward

"Good job" or "I agree" alone usually is not enough.

How many annotations do I need?

If an assignment requires annotations, the expectation is usually at least three thoughtful annotations. Fewer than three may be marked incomplete.

ENGL117 and ENGL&101

I am enrolled in ENGL&101 and ENGL117. What is the difference?

ENGL&101 is the main composition course. That is where major writing assignments live. ENGL117 is a support course. It is designed to help you succeed in ENGL&101 through extra practice, guidance, and structure.

Do I have to do the same work twice?

No. You will not write the same essay twice.

Some ENGL117 work supports what you are doing in ENGL&101. It is meant to make ENGL&101 easier, not add busywork.

What if I submitted something in the wrong course?

Email me right away and tell me which assignment, where you submitted it, and where it was supposed to go.

AI and academic integrity

Can I use AI tools for this class?

Sometimes, but it depends on how you use it. A simple rule is that AI can help you think, but AI cannot do the work for you.

What is usually okay?
  • Brainstorming topic ideas
  • Generating questions to help you think
  • Getting help understanding instructions, then you still do the work
  • Outlining after you have developed your own ideas
  • Editing support on your own writing for clarity and grammar
What is not okay?
  • Having AI write your discussion post, paragraph, or essay for you
  • Copying AI writing and turning it in as your own, even if you tweak it
  • Using AI to complete reading responses without actually reading
  • Using AI to create citations or sources you did not actually use

If you are not sure whether something counts, ask me before you submit.
